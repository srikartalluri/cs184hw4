Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

The effect of changing the spring constant changes how the cloth falls and hangs from the pinned state. More specifically, it changes how strong each spring between each point mass in the grid is. A low ks means the springs between the point masses are weak thus the force of gravity has an easier time pulling them apart. What this looks like in the simulation is that the cloth, in its hanging state, is more droopy. meaning more the point masses are more spread apart as the springs connecting them are stretched out more. This means the weight of the cloth that pulls down has more of an effect on each spring. Conversely, a high ks means the springs between the point masses are strong thus the force of gravity has a much harder time pulling them apart. In the simulation, the cloth in its hanging state is stiffer and looks more like cardboard. This means that the point masses are less spread apart as the springs connecting them are not stretched out as much.

What about for density?

Pretty much the opposite effect of varying ks.

The effect of changing density is that it essentially changes how heavy each point mass is. This given that the ks (how strong each spring is) is constant, we know that a higher density means that each point mass is now heavier. Thus every spring will be stretched out more. Imagine a spring holding a 10 pound vs a 100 pound weight. There is no doubt that the 100 pound weight will be stretched out more before reaching a resting state. This is the same effect here. By increasing the density, the force related to gravity for each point mass is more and thus will be pulled apart more. In simulation, this will cause the entire cloth to become more droopy in its hanging state. Conversely, a low density means that each of the pointmasses weighs less and thus each of the springs will now be more compressed. Thus overall, like with a high ks, the cloth will look more like cardboard with less droop.

What about for damping?

The damping variable changes places a “damping” factor on each position update for each point mass. Without damping, every point mass’s position gets updated according to a variety of factors and the new position calculated immediately becomes the position. The damping factor basically slows the updates by a linear factor.

In effect a high damping factor slows the updates of each point mass a lot. Thus the cloth falls a lot slower since every update is now less. Another thing to note is that it reaches its resting state relatively fast as it each update is now smaller. This is similar to how varying the step size in a gradient decent algorithm can help you reach your optimal value faster/slower. Conversely, a low (or 0) damping factor means the cloth falls much faster since we take (almost) the full value of the new calculated position. However, it also sways a lot more once it falls since there is acceleration associated with the force of the springs. Thus it keeps on swaying once it reaches the bottom and takes a very long time to converge to its final resting position.

Part 5: Shaders

Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects

A shader program is an isolated program that runs in parallel to the simulation on the GPU. It essentially tells how each point in each object in the simulation (whether it be a CollisionObject such as a sphere or plane or the cloth itself) should look. Taken overall, it determines how certain graphics are displayed on the screen, rather than having to compute them manually in the simulation.

The shader function is generally broken down into two steps. The first is vertex shaders. The vertex reads and writes all per-vertex values. It does this by processing each vertex of an object and/or interpolating based on actual given vertices. It can be seen in the .vert files that these files are responsible for taking in 3D positions in model space and ultimately transforming them into 2d screen space coordinates. It also has the ability to pass in various values (such as surface normals) to the next step of the pipeline which is fragment shaders.

Fragment shaders is responsible for actually writing all per-pixel values. As stated before, it can take in values from the vertex shader and computes the pixels final color. This final color can be based on multiple factors such as lighting, reflectivity, normals, and/or textures.

These two stages work together to create lighting and material effects by:

The vertex shader takes in various model space coordinates and adjusts them based on the camera position and perspective. It also stores some auxiliary info such as normals, tangents, uv vectors, position vectors, which are all passed to the fragment shader. These all get rasterized and then the fragment shader physically shades in all the colors of each pixel. All lighting, texture mapping, bump mapping, reflectivity, diffuse, etc, calculations are done here. Finally we are able to see various lighting and material effects on our screen as a result of the vertex and fragment programs working together.

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. 

The Blinn-Phong (phong) shading model works by describing the way an object should look under light by splitting the component of color into 3 different categories. The first is ambient lighting. This is general the baseline level of light present in the scene. The best way to think about this type of light is the light that exists without any specific light source present. This ensures that parts of the object that aren’t lit up by any light source are still visible.

The second component is the diffuse component which consists of any light that directly comes from a light source and scatters evenly around the incident point. It largely depends on the angle of incidence. If the angle is directly normal to the surface then the surface will appear bright and vice versa. This makes intuitive sense. This type of diffuse lighting gives the surface a matte finish.

The final component is the specular component, which is the shine component. This is largely dependent on the viewer's angle. The overall shininess is controlled by a shininess coefficient.

These 3 components are then added up and then outputted which makes up the phong shading model.

Compare the two approaches and resulting renders in your own words.

Bump mapping is a technique that simulates the depth of various textures by manipulating the surface normals to give the illusion of depth. It is important to note that it doesn’t actually change the geometry of the objects just the appearance of them. This can be seen in the renders as bump mapping still looks relatively smooth. It is even more pronounced when the cloth is laid over the sphere as it the shape of the relusting figure is identical to any previous shading.

However, displacement mapping works differently as it physically modifies the mesh / underlying surface geometry. This can be seen as we have to edit both the .vert and .frag files in order to implement displacement mapping. Similar to bump mapping, it find the surface normals. But unlike bump mapping, then displaces the points in the direction of their normals based on based on value of textures. This means the sphere, especially with the brick texture will now have actual grooves on it. These grooves and bricks are still apparent with the cloth laid over it as folds of the cloth lay just a little bit differently.
